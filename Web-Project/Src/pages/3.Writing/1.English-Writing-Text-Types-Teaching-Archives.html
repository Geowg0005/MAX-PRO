💡 ⚖️ Argumentative Essay (Persuasive Writing)

📊 Argumentative Slides

What is this?

An argumentative essay is when you write to persuade someone. You must:

  • Say what you believe about a topic
  • Give facts and examples to support your opinion
  • Be formal and logical (not emotional)

✓ Important Features

  • Tone (How you write): Formal, serious, logical - NOT emotional or casual
  • Tense (When): Use present tense for your opinion: "The evidence shows..." Use past tense for examples: "In 2020, this happened..."
  • Phrases to use: "In my view," "Research shows," "The data demonstrates," "It is clear that..."
  • ❌ DO NOT USE: Emotional words, "I think," "I feel," general statements without facts, slang

📋 How to Structure Your Essay - Follow This Plan

Paragraph 1: Introduction (First Paragraph - Say What You Believe)
  • Start with a Hook: Ask an interesting question or give an interesting fact to make readers interested
  • Give Background: Explain the topic briefly so readers understand the problem
  • Write Your Thesis: Write one sentence that says exactly what you believe. Example: "This essay argues that social media is harmful to young people."
Paragraphs 2-4: Body Paragraphs (Main Arguments - Give Facts)

Write 2-3 body paragraphs. Each paragraph should explain ONE argument.

  • Topic Sentence (First sentence): State ONE main argument or idea
  • Evidence: Give facts, numbers, examples, or quotations that prove your point
  • Explanation: Explain HOW the evidence supports your argument. Do not just give facts—explain why they are important
  • Conclusion: Connect this paragraph back to your main thesis
Paragraph 5: Counter-Argument (Opposite Opinion - Respond to Disagreement)

Acknowledge that some people disagree with you, then explain why they are wrong.

  • State the opposite view: "Some argue that..." or "Some people believe that..."
  • Explain why this view is weak: Show why this opinion is not correct or not strong
  • Show your argument is stronger: Explain why YOUR opinion is better
Final Paragraph: Conclusion (Paragraph 6 - Summarize and End)
  • Restate your thesis: Say your main belief again, but in different words
  • Summarize main arguments: Quickly remind readers of your 2-3 main points
  • End with power: Write a final sentence that makes an impact. Example: "Therefore, society must act now to address this issue."

💡 Argumentative Writing Tips

  • Start strong with your thesis: Make your position crystal clear from the start
    Readers should know exactly where you stand. This helps them follow your argument better.
  • Use facts, not feelings: Support every claim with evidence (statistics, studies, expert quotes)
    Facts convince people. Emotions might interest them, but only evidence changes minds.
  • Address counter-arguments: Show you've considered the other side
    This makes you look fair and thoughtful. It also strengthens your own argument.
  • Link paragraphs together: Use phrases like "Furthermore," "Additionally," "However"
    Transition words help readers follow your logic and see how ideas connect.
  • End with impact: Your conclusion should make readers think or act
    A strong ending stays in people's minds. Don't waste it by just repeating yourself.
  • Stay formal and objective: Avoid "I think" - use "Research shows" or "Evidence suggests"
    Formal language makes you sound more credible and trustworthy to readers.

💡 ✉️ Letter to the Editor

📊 Letter Format Guide

Purpose

To respond to a recent news article or issue and persuade readers and editors to consider your viewpoint.

Key Features

  • Tone: Formal, polite, but persuasive and critical
  • Length: 150-250 words (concise!)
  • Language: "I am writing to express concern," "I strongly believe," "It is imperative that..."
  • AVOID: Emotional outbursts, insults, offensive language

Structure & Format

Formatting (Physical Layout)
  • Your address (top left)
  • Date
  • Publication address
  • Salutation: "Dear Editor," or "Dear Sir/Madam,"
1. Opening (1 paragraph)
  • Reference: "In response to the article 'Your Article Title' published on [date]..."
  • Your Position: State your clear stance immediately
  • Tone: Polite but firm and direct
2. Body (1-2 paragraphs)
  • Reasoning: Explain why you agree or disagree
  • Evidence: Support with relevant facts or personal experience
  • Impact: Show how this issue affects the community
3. Closing (1 paragraph)
  • Action: Call for readers/officials to "Consider," "Act on," or "Reconsider"
  • Sign-off: "Yours faithfully," or "Sincerely,"
  • Full Name, Address, Contact (Required by publications)

💡 Letter to Editor Tips

  • Be brief and focused: Editors prefer letters under 250 words
    Short letters are more likely to get published. Every word should count.
  • Reference the article clearly: Mention the title and date in your first sentence
    This helps editors know exactly what you're responding to and shows you're informed.
  • Stay respectful but firm: Criticize ideas, not people
    Polite letters get published. Rude ones get thrown away, even if your point is valid.
  • Add local relevance: Connect the issue to your community
    Editors like letters that matter to their readers. Show why this affects real people.
  • Include your credentials: If relevant, mention why you're qualified to comment
    Being a teacher, student, or expert on the topic adds weight to your opinion.
  • End with a clear call to action: Tell readers or officials what to do next
    A strong ending gives purpose to your letter and shows you want real change.

💡 📋 Proposal (Request for Action)

📊 Proposal Templates

What is this?

A proposal is when you suggest a solution to a problem and convince decision-makers that your idea is practical and beneficial.

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Professional, optimistic, persuasive, polite
  • Format: Use headings, bullet points, organized layout
  • Audience: Decision-makers (principal, committee, manager, etc.)
  • Language: "I propose that...", "This initiative will...", "The benefits include..."
  • ❌ DO NOT: Use vague ideas, unrealistic timelines, ignore costs or challenges

📋 How to Structure Your Proposal

Section 1: Title & Introduction
  • Title: Give your proposal a clear, descriptive title. Example: "Proposal for a School Recycling Program"
  • Introduction: State the problem and briefly introduce your solution
  • Purpose: Explain why this proposal is needed right now
Section 2: Background/Problem
  • Explain the problem: What is wrong? Why does it need to be fixed?
  • Give evidence: Use facts, statistics, or observations to show the problem is real
  • Show impact: Explain who is affected by this problem
Section 3: Proposed Solution
  • Describe your idea clearly: What exactly are you proposing?
  • Be specific: Include details like timeline, location, people involved
  • Show it's realistic: Explain how it can actually be done
Section 4: Benefits & Impact
  • List all benefits: How will this help? Who will benefit?
  • Use bullet points: Make benefits easy to read
  • Connect to goals: Show how it aligns with school/organization goals
Section 5: Implementation Plan
  • Timeline: When will this happen? Include specific dates or phases
  • Resources needed: What materials, people, or budget is required?
  • Responsibilities: Who will do what?
Section 6: Conclusion
  • Summarize key points: Remind readers of the main benefits
  • Call to action: Ask for approval or support
  • Offer to discuss: "I would be happy to discuss this further..."

💡 Proposal Writing Tips

  • Make it look professional: Use clear headings, bullet points, and organized sections
    Good formatting shows you're serious and organized. It also makes reading easier for busy people.
  • Be specific with numbers: Include exact costs, timelines, and measurable goals
    Vague proposals get rejected. Specific details show you've thought everything through.
  • Show benefits clearly: List at least 3-5 concrete advantages
    Decision-makers need to see value. Make it obvious why they should say yes.
  • Address concerns upfront: Think about objections and answer them in your proposal
    This shows you're realistic and have planned for challenges. It builds trust.
  • Include a clear timeline: Break the project into phases with dates
    Timelines show the project is achievable and help people see progress will happen.
  • End positively: Express enthusiasm and offer to provide more information
    Positive energy is contagious. Show you're committed and easy to work with.

💡 📊 Report (Factual Writing)

📊 Report Writing Guide

What is this?

A report is factual writing that presents information objectively. You must:

  • Present facts and data without personal opinion
  • Organize information clearly with headings
  • Use formal, neutral language

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Objective, neutral, formal - NO personal opinions or emotions
  • Tense: Use past tense for events: "The survey was conducted..." Use present for findings: "The data shows..."
  • Language: "The findings indicate...", "According to...", "The results show...", "It was observed that..."
  • ❌ DO NOT USE: "I think", "I believe", emotional language, informal expressions

📋 How to Structure Your Report

Section 1: Title & Executive Summary
  • Title: Clear, descriptive title. Example: "Report on School Library Usage, 2025"
  • Executive Summary: Brief overview (2-3 sentences) of main findings
  • Date & Author: Include when and who wrote the report
Section 2: Introduction
  • Purpose: State why the report was written
  • Scope: What topics/areas does this report cover?
  • Methodology: How was information collected? (surveys, observations, etc.)
Section 3: Findings (Main Body)
  • Use headings: Divide information into clear sections with headings
  • Present data: Include statistics, numbers, facts, graphs if available
  • Be objective: Just present facts - don't give opinions yet
  • Use evidence: Support every statement with data or observations
Section 4: Analysis/Discussion
  • Interpret findings: What do the facts mean? What patterns emerge?
  • Make connections: How do different findings relate to each other?
  • Stay neutral: Analyze objectively, avoid bias
Section 5: Conclusion & Recommendations
  • Summarize key findings: What are the most important discoveries?
  • Recommendations: Based on facts, what actions should be taken?
  • Be practical: Suggest realistic, evidence-based solutions

💡 Report Writing Tips

  • Use headings and subheadings: Organize information into clearly labeled sections
    Headings help readers find specific information quickly. They also make long reports less scary.
  • Present facts without bias: Report what you found, not what you hoped to find
    Objectivity makes your report trustworthy. People rely on you to tell the truth.
  • Include numbers and data: Use specific statistics, percentages, and measurements
    Numbers make your findings concrete and credible. They're harder to argue with than opinions.
  • Use visual aids if possible: Tables, charts, or graphs make data clearer
    Pictures show patterns faster than words. They also break up text and keep readers engaged.
  • Separate findings from recommendations: Present facts first, then suggest actions
    This keeps your report organized and helps readers trust your analysis before your advice.
  • Write executive summary last: Summarize key points after finishing the full report
    You can't summarize what you haven't written yet. Save the summary for the very end.

💡 📰 Article (News/Feature Writing)

📊 Article Layout Tips

What is this?

An article is informative writing for newspapers or magazines. You must:

  • Catch the reader's attention immediately
  • Present information in an interesting, engaging way
  • Use a mix of facts and storytelling

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Engaging, lively, informative - can be slightly informal but still professional
  • Headline: Short, catchy, attention-grabbing (5-10 words)
  • Language: Active voice, vivid descriptions, quotes from people, varied sentence lengths
  • ❌ DO NOT: Use boring headlines, write long paragraphs, be too formal or academic

📋 How to Structure Your Article

Part 1: Headline & Lead Paragraph
  • Headline: Create a catchy, interesting title. Example: "Students Launch Green Initiative to Save Campus"
  • Lead Paragraph (First paragraph): Answer the 5 Ws: Who, What, When, Where, Why (and sometimes How)
  • Hook readers: Make the first sentence exciting or intriguing
Part 2: Body Paragraphs
  • Inverted Pyramid: Put most important information first, then details
  • Include quotes: Use what people said. Example: "This will change everything," says Student Council President Mary Chan.
  • Use facts & examples: Include numbers, dates, specific details
  • Keep paragraphs short: 2-4 sentences per paragraph for easy reading
  • Vary sentence length: Mix short punchy sentences with longer descriptive ones
Part 3: Conclusion
  • End with impact: Final quote, call to action, or looking forward
  • Don't just stop: Give readers a satisfying ending
  • Optional: Include a fact box or "Did You Know?" section

💡 Tips for Great Articles

  • Use active voice: "Students organized the event" not "The event was organized"
    Active voice is clearer and more direct. It makes your writing stronger and easier to read.
  • Show, don't just tell: Use descriptive details and examples
    Details and examples help readers understand and remember your article better.
  • Include human interest: Add personal stories or emotions
    Stories about real people make articles more interesting and help readers care about the topic.
  • Use subheadings for longer articles to break up text
    Subheadings help readers find information quickly and make long articles less scary to read.

💡 🎤 Speech (Oral Presentation Writing)

📊 Speech Delivery Slides

What is this?

A speech is spoken writing meant to be delivered to an audience. You must:

  • Connect with your audience emotionally
  • Use clear, memorable language
  • Organize ideas for easy listening (not reading)

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Conversational, engaging, passionate - can use "I", "we", "you"
  • Language: Simple, clear words; repetition for emphasis; rhetorical questions; direct address
  • Techniques: Use anecdotes (stories), facts, quotes, humor, emotion
  • ❌ DO NOT: Use complex vocabulary, long sentences, too many statistics, read like an essay

📋 How to Structure Your Speech

Part 1: Opening (Introduction)
  • Greeting: "Good morning, everyone" / "Dear fellow students"
  • Hook: Start with a question, quote, story, or shocking fact to grab attention
  • Preview: Tell the audience what you'll talk about: "Today I'll explain three reasons why..."
Part 2: Body (Main Points)
  • 3 Main Points: Organize speech around 2-4 key ideas (3 is ideal)
  • Signpost: Use clear transitions: "First...", "Secondly...", "Finally..."
  • Support each point: Use examples, stories, statistics, or quotes
  • Repeat key phrases: Repetition helps listeners remember
  • Use "Rule of 3": List things in threes for impact. Example: "We need courage, commitment, and compassion."
Part 3: Conclusion
  • Summary: Briefly remind audience of your main points
  • Call to action: What do you want the audience to do or think?
  • Memorable ending: End with a powerful quote, question, or image
  • Thank audience: "Thank you for your time and attention"

💡 Speech Writing Tips

  • Use "we" and "you": Make it personal and inclusive
    These words make the audience feel included. They listen better when you speak to them directly.
  • Ask rhetorical questions: "Have you ever wondered why...?"
    Questions make people think and stay focused on what you're saying.
  • Tell stories: Personal anecdotes make speeches memorable
    Stories are easier to remember than facts. They help people connect with your message.
  • Use pauses: Mark places to pause for emphasis: [PAUSE]
    Pauses give your audience time to think about important points. They also make you sound confident.
  • Practice saying it aloud: Speeches should sound natural when spoken
    Writing and speaking are different. Reading aloud helps you find and fix awkward parts.

💡 📖 Story (Narrative Writing)

📊 Narrative Plot Arc Guide

What is this?

A story is creative writing that tells about characters and events. You must:

  • Create interesting characters and settings
  • Build a plot with a beginning, middle, and end
  • Use descriptive language to show, not tell

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Creative, descriptive, engaging - can vary based on story mood
  • Tense: Usually past tense ("She walked...") or present ("She walks...") - stay consistent!
  • Language: Vivid descriptions, dialogue, sensory details (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)
  • ❌ DO NOT: Tell everything, use clichés, switch tenses randomly, forget to show emotions

📖 Freytag's Pyramid - Story Structure Map!

📚 Exposition: Introduce characters & setting → 🚀 Rising Action: Build tension & conflict → ⭐ Climax: The BIG moment! → 💫 Falling Action: After the peak → 🎉 Resolution: Wrap it up!

📋 How to Structure Your Story

Part 1: Beginning (Introduction/Exposition)
  • Hook the reader: Start with action, dialogue, or interesting description
  • Introduce: Main character, setting (where and when), situation
  • Set the tone: Is it scary? Funny? Sad? Make this clear early
Part 2: Middle (Rising Action & Climax)
  • Rising Action: Build tension - problems get bigger, stakes get higher
  • Climax: The most exciting/important moment - the turning point
  • Show character development: How does the character change or react?
  • Use dialogue: Let characters speak to reveal personality and move plot forward
Part 3: End (Falling Action & Resolution)
  • Falling Action: After the climax, things wind down
  • Resolution: How does the story end? What happens to characters?
  • Satisfying conclusion: Tie up loose ends, but don't over-explain

💡 Story Writing Tips

  • Show, don't tell: Instead of "She was scared," write "Her hands trembled as she reached for the door."
    This helps readers see and feel what's happening. It makes the story more interesting.
  • Use the five senses: Describe what characters see, hear, smell, taste, touch
    This makes your story feel real. Readers can imagine they are in the story.
  • Vary sentence length: Short sentences create tension. Longer ones slow the pace.
    Short sentences make exciting parts faster. Long sentences slow down calm parts.
  • Use strong verbs: "She sprinted" is better than "She ran quickly"
    One strong word is clearer than many weak words. It saves space and sounds better.
  • Create conflict: Every good story needs a problem or challenge
    Problems make readers want to know what happens next. No problem = boring story.
  • Make dialogue realistic: How people actually talk (contractions, interruptions, etc.)
    Real-sounding dialogue makes characters feel like real people, not robots.

💡 💻 Blog Post (Online Opinion/Personal Writing)

📊 Blog SEO & Engagement Guide

What is this?

A blog post is informal online writing where you share opinions, experiences, or advice. You can:

  • Use a casual, conversational tone
  • Share personal opinions and experiences
  • Engage directly with readers

✓ Important Features

  • Tone: Casual, friendly, conversational - can use "I" and "you" freely
  • Format: Short paragraphs, subheadings, bullet points, images
  • Language: Contractions okay (I'm, don't), questions to readers, personal pronouns
  • ❌ DO NOT: Be too formal, use very long paragraphs, ignore your audience

📋 How to Structure Your Blog Post

Part 1: Title & Opening
  • Catchy Title: Use numbers, questions, or bold statements. Example: "5 Ways to Ace Your Exams" or "Why I Stopped Using Social Media"
  • Hook: Start with a question, personal story, or bold statement
  • Preview: Tell readers what they'll learn: "In this post, I'll share..."
Part 2: Body (Main Content)
  • Use subheadings: Break content into sections with descriptive headings
  • Short paragraphs: 2-4 sentences max - easier to read online
  • Lists & bullets: Use bullet points and numbered lists frequently
  • Personal examples: Share your own experiences and stories
  • Ask questions: "Have you ever felt this way?" engages readers
Part 3: Conclusion & Call to Action
  • Summarize key points: Remind readers of main takeaways
  • Call to action: "Try this today!" or "What do you think? Comment below!"
  • Invite engagement: Ask readers to share, comment, or try something

💡 Blog Writing Tips

  • Write like you talk: Be conversational and authentic
    Friendly, natural writing makes readers feel like they're having a conversation with you.
  • Use "you" often: Speak directly to your reader
    This makes your blog feel personal and helps readers feel you're talking to them.
  • Add personality: Let your voice and opinions shine through
    Your unique style is what makes people want to read YOUR blog instead of others.
  • Make it scannable: Use headings, bold text, short paragraphs so readers can skim
    Most people scan before reading. Good formatting helps them find what they want quickly.
  • Tell stories: Personal anecdotes make blogs relatable
    Personal stories help readers connect with you and remember your message better.
  • Be helpful or entertaining: Give value to your readers
    People read blogs to learn something or enjoy themselves. Give them a reason to come back.
